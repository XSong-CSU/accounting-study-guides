How to use this workflow

Designed for both class demonstration and solo practice.

Chapter 2 valued a combination on day one. Chapter 3 asks what happens after the acquisition, once time has passed: the subsidiary earns income, pays dividends, and the acquisition-date fair-value adjustments slowly amortize. Work the boxes in order. Every box has three parts: the textbook rule, a place to write your own numbers, and a "Show the method" button that explains how to get the figure — the formula and the decision rule, but never the final answer.

The big idea (textbook). A parent may keep its Investment in Subsidiary account three different ways internally — the equity, initial value, or partial equity method — but the consolidated statements come out identical either way (LO 3-4). The internal method only changes which worksheet entries you need to get there. Master the worksheet sequence and the choice of method stops being scary.
The two engines that drive every subsequent-period worksheet. (1) The subsidiary's book value changes over time — income added, dividends subtracted — so the equity you eliminate is the beginning-of-current-year balance, not the original. (2) The acquisition-date excess fair value amortizes each year, so the allocations you bring onto the worksheet shrink, and a fresh amortization expense appears every period.

Identify the parent's internal method first TEXTBOOK

LO 3-2 & LO 3-3 — equity vs. initial value vs. partial equity

Before touching the worksheet, decide how the parent has been carrying its Investment in Subsidiary account internally. This single fact decides whether you need an Entry *C and how Entry I behaves. Click the method the problem tells you the parent uses.

Equity method. Parent accrues its share of subsidiary income as earned, records the excess-fair-value amortization, and reduces the Investment for dividends. Account used: "Equity in subsidiary earnings."
Initial value (cost) method. Parent leaves the Investment frozen at acquisition-date cost and records only Dividend income when the subsidiary declares dividends. No income accrual, no amortization on the parent's books.
Partial equity method. Parent accrues its share of subsidiary income as earned, but records no amortization and no deferrals. Account used: "Equity in subsidiary earnings," before amortization.
Simplest path. Under the equity method the parent's books already carry full-accrual figures, so no Entry *C is needed. Go straight to the S·A·I·D·E sequence. Entry I removes "Equity in subsidiary earnings" (income net of amortization).
Needs conversion. Add an Entry *C to rebuild the parent's beginning Retained Earnings as if the equity method had always been used (prior-year income accruals and prior-year amortizations were never booked). And because the parent recorded Dividend income instead of an income accrual, Entry I eliminates Dividend income — and no separate Entry D is then needed.
Needs partial conversion. Add an Entry *C, but it only fixes the prior-year amortizations the parent skipped (income was already accrued). Entry I still removes "Equity in subsidiary earnings," and Entry D still removes the intra-entity dividends.
Whichever method you pick, the consolidated totals are identical (LO 3-4). The method only changes the route — specifically, whether *C appears and how I and D are written. Steps 1–3 below feed the worksheet the same way for all three methods, so do them once.

How the textbook builds this schedule

Excess = Consideration transferred − Book value of subsidiary's net assets. Then assign the excess account by account: each identifiable asset or liability gets the difference between its acquisition-date fair value and its book value, including intangibles the subsidiary never recorded. The amount still unassigned after all identifiable items is goodwill.

  • If the identifiable adjustments use up the entire excess, goodwill is zero — a perfectly valid outcome, not an error.
  • A fair value below book value is a negative allocation (it will later reduce expense, not add to it).
  • This schedule is fixed on day one. In every later year you re-use it; you do not recompute the excess from new balances.

How the textbook builds this figure

Annual amortization = Allocation ÷ Remaining useful life for every finite-lived item. Add them (a negative allocation contributes a negative amount, lowering the total). Points the chapter stresses:

  • Goodwill is never amortized — it is tested for impairment instead. Leave its life blank so it contributes nothing here.
  • Land and other indefinite-lived intangibles are also not amortized.
  • This single total becomes Entry E every year, and it is the amount that makes consolidated net income differ from the simple sum of the two companies' incomes.
  • A negative equipment allocation produces a credit to depreciation expense (it reduces expense) — keep its sign.

How the textbook builds these balances

Unamortized at start of current year = Original allocation − (Annual amortization × full years already elapsed). For a December year-end with a January 1 acquisition, "years already elapsed" is the number of complete years before the current one (e.g., consolidating 2024 for a 1/1/2022 acquisition → two prior years, 2022 and 2023).

  • Goodwill carries unchanged — no amortization, so its unamortized balance equals its original allocation every year.
  • These declining balances are exactly what Entry A brings onto the worksheet each year (Exhibit 3.8).
  • Do not confuse this beginning-of-year figure (Entry A) with the current-year expense (Entry E). A handles the cumulative leftover; E handles this year's slice.

What *C corrects, by method

  • Initial value method. The parent never accrued income and never amortized. *C adds the full prior-period effect: (prior-years' subsidiary income − dividends) − prior-years' excess amortization. Debit Investment, credit beginning Retained Earnings for that net amount.
  • Partial equity method. The parent already accrued income but skipped amortization. *C only removes the prior-years' amortization: debit beginning Retained Earnings, credit Investment. Note the direction flips versus initial value.
  • Equity method. Nothing to convert — no *C entry exists.

Self-check: *C only ever touches prior periods. The current year's income, dividends, and amortization are handled by Entries I, D, and E — never by *C.

The logic behind the letters (textbook)

  • S removes the subsidiary's beginning equity so it is not double-counted, and offsets the book-value slice of the Investment account.
  • A replaces that book value with the subsidiary's assets/liabilities at the unamortized fair-value amounts, plus any goodwill — using the beginning-of-year balances from Step 3.
  • I removes the income the parent recorded on its own books this year, so consolidated revenue/expense reflect the subsidiary's actual accounts, not a one-line accrual.
  • D removes the subsidiary's dividend declaration that the parent collected, so it is not counted as both a reduction of the Investment and a distribution.
  • E records this year's slice of excess amortization on the fair-value adjustments — the expense that exists only at the consolidated level.
  • P nets out any amounts the two companies owe each other, which are not real receivables/payables for the combined entity.

Self-check: after S + A + I + D (and *C), the Investment in Subsidiary account should net to zero in the consolidated column. If it does not, an amount is wrong or an entry is missing.

The payoff check: same totals, every method TEXTBOOK

LO 3-4 — a parent's internal method never changes the consolidated result

This is both the chapter's headline idea and your best error-catcher. Whatever internal method the parent used, the consolidated statements are identical — the entries just differ on the way there. Two checks confirm you arrived correctly.

Verify this……and it confirms
The Investment in Subsidiary account nets to zero after *C, S, A, I, D.You eliminated the full investment (both its book-value and fair-value portions).
The "Equity in subsidiary earnings" / "Dividend income" line nets to zero.The parent's internal income figure was fully removed; only the subsidiary's real accounts remain.
Consolidated net income = parent's own income + subsidiary's income − excess amortization.Entry E's amount is the only difference from a naive sum of the two incomes.
If you redid the problem under a different internal method and got a different consolidated total, something is wrong — not a feature of the method. The total is method-invariant; only *C, I, and D change form.

Reliability checklist STUDY AID

A self-verification routine — habits that catch the common errors

The textbook gives the method; this checklist is a general study habit to make it reliable. Tick each item before you call the problem done.

0 of 8 complete
  • I identified the parent's internal method (equity / initial value / partial equity) before writing any entry.
  • I reconstructed the day-one allocation schedule and let goodwill fall out as the residual (even if it is zero).
  • My amortization schedule excludes goodwill and any indefinite-lived items.
  • Entry A uses the beginning-of-year unamortized balances, not the original allocations.
  • I added Entry *C only for initial value / partial equity, and used the correct direction.
  • Entry I matches the method (equity earnings vs. dividend income), and Entry D is present/absent accordingly.
  • Each entry balances, and the Investment account nets to zero after *C, S, A, I, D.
  • I sanity-checked that the consolidated totals would be the same under any internal method (LO 3-4).
