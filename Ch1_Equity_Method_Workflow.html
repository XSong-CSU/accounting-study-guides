How to use this workflow

Designed for both class demonstration and solo practice.

The equity method is a single-line consolidation: one Investment account on the investor's books captures everything that would otherwise be spread across a full consolidation. Work the boxes in order. Each has the textbook rule, a place for your own numbers, and a "Show the method" button that explains how a figure is built — the formula and the rule, never the final answer.

The big idea (textbook). With significant influence (normally 20%–50% ownership), the investor recognizes income as the investee earns it — not when dividends arrive. Each year the Investment account moves: up for your share of investee income, down for dividends and for amortization of the premium you paid, and adjusted for any unrealized intra-entity profit.
The master formula (the one thing to memorize). Equity in investee income = (% × investee net income) − excess fair-value amortization − current-year intra-entity profit deferred + prior-year intra-entity profit now realized. Every box below builds one piece of this.

Decide which method applies TEXTBOOK

LO 1-2 & LO 1-3 — the sole criterion is significant influence

Before any computation, decide how the investment is reported. The driver is the degree of influence, usually signalled by ownership percentage. Click the level that matches.

Less than 20% — no significant influence. Fair-value method (or cost method if no readily determinable fair value).
20%–50% — significant influence. Equity method (the fair-value option is also permitted).
More than 50% (or control) — control. Consolidation (Chapters 2–5), not the equity method.
Fair value / cost. Income = dividends received; the investment is carried at fair value (when available). This is how Red Hawk's original 10% was reported in 2022.
Equity method. Recognize your share of investee income as earned; adjust for amortization and intra-entity profit; dividends reduce the Investment account. This is the rest of this workflow.
Consolidation. Combine the two companies line by line — see the Chapter 2–5 workflows.
Percentage is only a guideline; the real test is the ability to exercise significant influence. Indicators include board representation, participation in policy-making, material intra-entity transactions, interchange of managerial personnel, and technological dependency. A 30% holder who can't actually influence may still be excluded; an under-20% holder who demonstrably can may qualify.
For Red Hawk / Wolf: the 10% interest in 2022 was fair value; the second purchase in 2023 lifts ownership to 30% and grants significant influence, triggering the switch to the equity method.

How the textbook builds this (LO 1-5)

Excess = Cost − (Ownership % × investee book value of net assets). Then for each asset whose fair value exceeds book value, your allocation is investee undervaluation × your %. Whatever is left after all identifiable allocations is goodwill.

  • Only your ownership share of each undervaluation is allocated — you bought a percentage, not the whole asset.
  • Goodwill is not amortized (indefinite life); land and other indefinite-lived items aren't either.
  • This schedule is fixed when significant influence begins; you reuse it every year to compute amortization (next box).

Amortize the excess allocations TEXTBOOK

LO 1-5 — the annual charge that reduces equity income

Each finite-lived allocation is written off over the asset's remaining life. This annual amortization reduces the equity income you recognize (and the Investment account). Goodwill is never amortized.

How the textbook builds this (LO 1-5)

Annual amortization = Allocation ÷ remaining life for each finite-lived item; goodwill contributes nothing. The annual entry is Dr Equity in Investee Income / Cr Investment — so amortization lowers both your reported income and the carrying amount of the investment.

Defer unrealized intra-entity gross profit TEXTBOOK

LO 1-7 — profit on goods still “in the family” isn't earned yet

When the investor and investee trade inventory at a profit and some is still held at year-end, the investor's share of that unrealized gross profit must be deferred until the goods are sold to outsiders. Under the equity method, downstream and upstream sales are treated the same way.

How the textbook builds this (LO 1-7)

Deferral = gross profit rate × intra-entity inventory still held × ownership %. The entry is Dr Equity in Investee Income / Cr Investment in the year of the sale; the following year, when the goods reach outsiders, you reverse it (Dr Investment / Cr Equity in Investee Income) to recognize the now-realized profit.

  • Downstream = investor sells to investee; upstream = investee sells to investor. Under the equity method both use the investor's ownership %.
  • Only the portion still held at year-end is deferred; whatever the buyer already resold to outsiders is fully earned.

The master formula: equity in investee income TEXTBOOK

LO 1-4 → 1-7 — assemble the four pieces

The full computation (LO 1-4 → 1-7)

Equity in investee income = (% × investee NI) − excess amortization − current deferral + prior realization.

  • Dividends are not here. Investee dividends never affect income under the equity method — they reduce the Investment account (next box). Income was already recognized when the investee earned it.
  • Other comprehensive income is recognized separately: your share of the investee's OCI increases the Investment account and your own OCI, not net income.
  • This single figure is what posts to "Equity in Investee Income" on the income statement.

Roll the Investment account forward TEXTBOOK

LO 1-4 — one account captures everything

The Investment account is the single line that ties it all together. Start at cost and apply each effect for the year.

Investment, beginning of year (at cost)
+ Equity in investee income (Step 4)
+ Your share of investee other comprehensive income (% × OCI)
− Your share of investee dividends (% × dividends)
= Investment, end of year—

How the account rolls forward (LO 1-4)

Ending Investment = beginning + equity income + share of OCI − share of dividends. Note the equity-income figure already absorbs amortization and intra-entity deferrals (Step 4), so they don't appear again here. Dividends reduce the balance because they return part of the investee's net assets to you — income was recognized earlier, when earned.

Reliability checklist STUDY AID

A self-verification routine — habits that catch the common errors

The textbook gives the method; this checklist makes it reliable. Tick each before calling the problem done.

0 of 8 complete
  • I confirmed the equity method applies (significant influence, normally 20%–50%) before using it.
  • Excess = cost − (% × investee book value), and I allocated only my ownership share of each undervaluation.
  • Goodwill (the residual) was not amortized; only finite-lived allocations were.
  • I recognized my share of investee income as earned — not dividends — as income.
  • Dividends reduced the Investment account; they never hit income.
  • Amortization and current intra-entity deferral reduced equity income; prior-year deferral was added back.
  • My share of investee OCI went to the Investment account and my own OCI, not net income.
  • My Investment roll-forward ties out: beginning + income + OCI − dividends = ending.
