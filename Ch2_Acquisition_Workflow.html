How to use this workflow

Designed for both class demonstration and solo practice.

Work the seven boxes in order. Every box has three parts: the textbook rule (what the chapter tells you to do), a place to write your own numbers, and a "Show the method" button that explains how to get the figure — the formula and the decision rule, but never the final answer. The same routine works for any acquisition problem; only the numbers change.

The big idea (textbook). The acquisition method values a combination from the acquirer's point of view in three measurements: (1) the consideration transferred, (2) the net identifiable assets acquired at fair value, and (3) the difference between them, which is either goodwill or a gain on bargain purchase. Master those three and every variation in the chapter falls into place.

Classify the problem before you compute TEXTBOOK

LO 2-6 & LO 2-7 — dissolution vs. separate incorporation

The textbook stresses that the legal form of the combination decides which procedure you use. Decide this first — it tells you whether you make a journal entry on the parent's books, build a consolidation worksheet, or both.

Is the acquired company dissolved? (it stops being a separate legal entity) → record assets & liabilities directly on the parent's books via a journal entry. (This is requirement a.1.)
Does the acquired company keep separate incorporation? (it keeps its own books) → no permanent merger of records; you build a consolidation worksheet each period. (This is requirement a.2.)
Path A applies. Use the acquisition-method journal entry (Steps 1–3, then the entry in the "a.1 Dissolution" box). Subsidiary balances are absorbed permanently.
Path B applies. Use the six-step worksheet (Exhibit 2.6) in the "a.2 Worksheet" box. The companies' books stay separate; consolidation happens only on the worksheet.
This problem asks for both. Requirement a.1 = dissolution (journal entries); requirement a.2 = separate incorporation (worksheet). Steps 1–3 below feed both paths, so do them once and reuse the results.

How the textbook builds this figure

Consideration transferred = the fair value of all consideration the acquirer surrenders: cash, other assets, and stock issued measured at its fair value (shares × FV per share), plus the fair value of contingent consideration (recorded as a liability when payable in cash, or in equity when payable in stock).

Watch-outs from the chapter:

  • Use the fair value of the contingency (its estimated/measured value), not the maximum dollar amount that might eventually be paid.
  • Stock-issuance / registration costs are NOT part of consideration — they reduce Additional Paid-in Capital (see the Costs box).
  • Accounting, legal, and other direct combination costs are NOT part of consideration — they are expensed when incurred.

Net identifiable assets at fair value—

How the textbook builds this figure

Under the acquisition method, you recognize each identifiable asset acquired and liability assumed at its acquisition-date fair value — independently of what the consideration was. Three points the chapter emphasizes:

  • Fair value, not book value. Ignore the subsidiary's carrying amounts; use the fair-value column.
  • Recognize previously unrecorded intangibles if they are identifiable (e.g., unpatented technology, in-process R&D, trademarks) even though they were never on the subsidiary's books.
  • Subtract liabilities assumed at fair value. The result is "net identifiable assets at fair value." It is not goodwill yet.

Enter figures in Steps 1 and 2 to see which rule applies.

The three outcomes (Exhibit 2.4)

  • Consideration > net identifiable FV → record GOODWILL (a debit asset) for the excess.
  • Consideration = net identifiable FV → no goodwill and no gain.
  • Consideration < net identifiable FV → record a GAIN ON BARGAIN PURCHASE for the shortfall (the acquirer paid less than the net fair value received). The chapter notes bargain purchases are uncommon and warrant re-checking your fair-value measurements before concluding.

How the dissolution entry is built (textbook)

  • Debit each identifiable asset at fair value; credit each liability at fair value.
  • Debit Goodwill if consideration exceeded net FV (Step 3 positive); credit Gain on bargain purchase if it was less (Step 3 negative).
  • Credit the consideration: Common Stock at par (shares × par), Additional Paid-in Capital for the fair value above par, and a Contingent Performance Liability at its fair value.
  • Separately, record the two cost entries: debit an expense for the $20,000 direct costs (credit Cash); debit APIC for the $10,000 stock-issuance costs (credit Cash).

Self-check: total debits must equal total credits, and the net amount credited for stock + contingency should equal your Step 1 consideration.

Path B — Separate incorporation: the six-step worksheet TEXTBOOK

LO 2-7, Exhibit 2.6 — Richmond keeps its own books, so consolidate on a worksheet

When the subsidiary survives, no merger of records occurs; you consolidate on a worksheet each period. The chapter lays out exactly six steps — follow them in order.

StepWhat you do (textbook)
1Fair-value allocation schedule. Consideration transferred (Step 1) − subsidiary's book value of net assets = excess; allocate the excess to specific assets/liabilities for their FV − BV differences; any remainder is goodwill.
2Enter the separate balances. Place each company's account balances in their own columns (income statement, then retained earnings, then balance sheet).
3Entry S. Eliminate the subsidiary's stockholders' equity (Common Stock, APIC, and Retained Earnings as of the acquisition date) against the Investment account. For a date-of-acquisition worksheet whose acquisition falls on the year-end, that means the subsidiary's 12/31 retained earnings, not its 1/1 balance.
4Entry A. Recognize the fair-value allocations from Step 1 (assign the excess to the specific assets, intangibles, and goodwill) against the remaining Investment balance.
5Add across. For each row: parent + subsidiary ± consolidation entries = consolidated total.
6Combine vertically. Revenues − expenses = net income; carry net income to the retained-earnings statement; carry ending retained earnings to the balance sheet.

Why Entry S and Entry A work (textbook)

Entry S removes the subsidiary's stockholders' equity at the acquisition date so it isn't double-counted, and offsets the portion of the Investment account that represents book value. (On a date-of-acquisition worksheet the subsidiary's own pre-acquisition revenues, expenses, and beginning retained earnings are not consolidated, so consolidated revenues and retained earnings 1/1 come from the parent only.) Entry A adds the fair-value increments and goodwill identified in the Step 1 schedule, offsetting the rest of the Investment account. Together they reduce the parent's Investment account to zero on the worksheet, replacing it with the subsidiary's actual assets and liabilities at fair value. After the entries, add across (Step 5) and combine vertically (Step 6) to reach consolidated totals.

Self-check: after Entry S + Entry A, the Investment account should net to zero in the consolidated column.

Part (b) — Re-run with a $26 share fair value TEXTBOOK

Sensitivity: what changes when the consideration falls?

Don't re-do the whole problem. Only one input changed (fair value per share), so only the figures that depend on it can change. Re-run the relevant steps:

  1. Step 1 again. Recompute consideration transferred with $26 instead of $32. (Net identifiable FV in Step 2 does not change.)
  2. Step 3 again. Re-compare the new consideration to the unchanged net identifiable FV, and re-read the decision rule. The sign of the difference may flip.
  3. Describe the effect. State how the consolidated balances differ from part (a) given the new outcome of the Step 3 comparison.

The reasoning (rule only)

Lowering the per-share fair value lowers the consideration transferred while the net identifiable fair value stays the same. Whenever consideration drops below net identifiable fair value, Step 3's rule changes the outcome: the excess that would have been recorded as goodwill shrinks, and if consideration falls below net FV you instead record a gain on bargain purchase. Trace that single change through the journal entry (Path A) or the Step 1 allocation schedule and Entry A (Path B) and describe the resulting differences. Use your own recomputed figures — the rule, not the number, is the point.

Reliability checklist STUDY AID

A self-verification routine — habits that catch the common errors

The textbook gives the method; this checklist is a general study habit to make the method reliable. Tick each item before you call the problem done.

0 of 8 complete
  • I classified the combination (dissolution vs. separate incorporation) before computing.
  • Consideration uses the fair value of the contingency, not the maximum payout.
  • Stock-issuance costs went to APIC; direct (legal/accounting) costs were expensed — neither touched consideration.
  • Net identifiable assets use fair values, including previously unrecorded intangibles.
  • I compared consideration to net FV and applied the correct rule (goodwill vs. bargain gain).
  • Dissolution entry: total debits = total credits.
  • Worksheet: Entry S + Entry A reduce the Investment account to zero.
  • Part (b): I re-ran only the share-dependent steps and re-checked the Step 3 sign.
