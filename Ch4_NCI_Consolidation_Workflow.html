How to use this workflow

Designed for both class demonstration and solo practice.

Chapter 3 consolidated a wholly owned subsidiary. Chapter 4 changes one thing with big consequences: the parent owns less than 100%. The slice it does not own is the noncontrolling interest (NCI) — real outside owners who get their share of the subsidiary's equity, income, and dividends. Almost everything from Chapter 3 still applies; you simply split the subsidiary between the controlling and noncontrolling interests at the right moments. Boxes tagged NCI are exactly those moments.

The big idea (textbook). Under the acquisition (economic-unit) method, you value and report 100% of the subsidiary at acquisition-date fair value — even the part you didn't buy. The subsidiary's total fair value = the parent's consideration + the acquisition-date fair value of the NCI. Goodwill, asset write-ups, and amortization are all recorded in full, then the NCI is assigned its ownership-percentage share.
Four NCI figures to keep straight (LO 4-5). (1) NCI at the beginning of the year; (2) NCI's share of net income (after excess amortization); (3) NCI's share of dividends; (4) NCI at the end of the year. Get these four right and the worksheet's NCI column takes care of itself.

1

Value the whole subsidiary: total acquisition-date fair value NCI

LO 4-2 — the economic-unit starting point

When ownership is below 100%, you still value all of the subsidiary. Add the parent's consideration to the acquisition-date fair value of the NCI. This combined figure — not just the price the parent paid — drives the excess and goodwill in Step 2.

Consideration transferred by parent (controlling interest)
+ Acquisition-date fair value of the noncontrolling interest
= Total acquisition-date fair value of the subsidiary—

How the textbook builds this figure

Total fair value = Consideration transferred + Fair value of NCI. The controlling piece is easy — the consideration the parent paid is the best evidence of its fair value. The NCI piece has no purchase to point to, so the chapter measures it at fair value using market evidence (e.g., the trading price of the shares the parent did not buy).

  • If the NCI's per-share value equals the parent's per-share price, there is no control premium and goodwill later splits proportionately.
  • If the parent paid a control premium, the NCI per-share value is lower, and goodwill will not split by ownership percentage (Step 3 handles this).

How the textbook builds this schedule

Excess = Total fair value − Book value of net assets, then assign account by account at full fair-value differences, residual to goodwill. Because you started from the total (controlling + NCI) fair value, the write-ups and goodwill are automatically at 100% — the economic-unit view.

  • Land and goodwill are not amortized; finite-lived items are (Step 4).
  • A zero or negative residual can signal a bargain purchase on the parent's portion — re-check the fair values first.
  • This is the same allocation logic as Chapters 2–3; only the starting total changed.

How each goodwill figure is built (textbook)

Controlling goodwill = Consideration − (Controlling % × identifiable net-asset FV)
NCI goodwill = NCI fair value − (NCI % × identifiable net-asset FV)

  • No control premium: NCI per-share value matches the parent's, and the two goodwill figures end up proportional to ownership.
  • Control premium: the premium accrues only to the controlling interest, so its goodwill is disproportionately large and the NCI's is smaller. This is exactly why you don't just multiply total goodwill by 20%.
  • It is perfectly possible for NCI goodwill to be zero (when NCI fair value equals its share of identifiable net assets) while controlling goodwill is positive.
  • The two pieces should sum to the Step 2 total — a built-in cross-check.

How the textbook builds this figure

Annual amortization = Allocation ÷ Remaining life for each finite-lived item; leave indefinite items (land, goodwill) blank. This single total is what makes consolidated income differ from the simple sum of the two companies — and, crucially in Chapter 4, it reduces the subsidiary's adjusted income before you carve off the NCI's share.

  • Watch the lives: an item with a 5-year life acquired five years ago is fully amortized by now — its current-year expense may be its last.
  • Keep signs: a negative allocation reduces expense.

How the textbook builds these balances

  • These beginning balances feed Entry A, and the NCI's share of their total is part of beginning NCI (NCI box below).

What *C corrects, by method

  • Initial value. Add the parent's share of the prior-years' net effect: controlling % × (subsidiary income − dividends, prior years) − controlling % × prior-years' excess amortization. Debit Investment, credit beginning Retained Earnings.
  • Partial equity. Remove only the prior-years' amortization (parent's share): debit beginning Retained Earnings, credit Investment.
  • Equity. No *C.

Self-check: *C uses the parent's ownership % only, and touches only prior periods. The current year flows through Entries I, D, and E.

What's different from the 100% case (textbook)

  • S — the subsidiary's full beginning equity comes out, but only the parent's % offsets the Investment; the rest is credited to the Noncontrolling Interest column (the NCI's share of book value).
  • A — the full unamortized excess and goodwill go onto the books, offset to Investment for the parent's % and to Noncontrolling Interest for the NCI's % (the NCI's share of the fair-value step-up).
  • I — unchanged in spirit: it removes only the parent's recorded income from the subsidiary.
  • D — only the parent's share of dividends is eliminated; the NCI's share is a real distribution to outside owners and reduces ending NCI.
  • E — full amortization is recorded; the NCI automatically bears its share because amortization lowers the subsidiary's adjusted income that feeds the NCI line.
  • P — unaffected by ownership %; intra-entity balances net to zero regardless of who owns what.

Self-check: after *C, S, A, I, D the Investment account nets to zero; the new Noncontrolling Interest column accumulates beginning NCI + income share − dividend share = ending NCI (next box).

How the textbook builds these (LO 4-4 / 4-5)

  • NCI share of income = NCI % × (subsidiary net income − excess amortization). Use the adjusted subsidiary income, not its raw income, because the fair-value amortization belongs to the consolidated entity.
  • NCI roll-forward: Ending NCI = Beginning NCI + NCI income share − NCI dividend share. The beginning NCI itself = NCI % of the subsidiary's beginning book value + NCI % of the beginning unamortized excess (Steps 5 + 3).
  • Income split: consolidated net income − NCI's share = the controlling (parent) share. Both appear on the consolidated income statement; the NCI line is a deduction, not an expense.
  • Cross-check: rolling forward from the beginning balance should match 20% of the subsidiary's ending book value plus 20% of the ending unamortized excess — two routes, one answer.
