How to use this workflow

Designed for both class demonstration and solo practice.

A consolidated entity can't make a profit selling to itself. When a parent and subsidiary transfer inventory, land, or equipment, any gain recorded on the affiliate's books is unrealized until that asset is finally sold to an outside party. Chapter 5 is a catalog of how to defer that profit now and recognize it later. Work the boxes by transfer type; each has the textbook rule, a calculator, and a "Show the method" button.

The big idea (textbook). Every intra-entity transfer needs two things on the worksheet: (1) undo the artificial accounts the affiliates recorded (intra-entity sales/COGS, an intra-entity gain, an inflated asset basis), and (2) defer the unrealized profit until the year the asset is consumed or sold outside — at which point it becomes real and is recognized.
Two questions answer almost everything. For each transfer ask: (1) Which direction? Downstream (parent→sub) keeps the profit with the controlling interest; upstream (sub→parent) shares it with the NCI. (2) What type of asset? Inventory (realized when resold/consumed), land (deferral persists with no depreciation), or a depreciable asset (deferral unwinds gradually via excess depreciation).

LO 5-5 — direction drives the NCI; type drives how the deferral unwinds

Direction

Downstream — parent sells to subsidiary. Profit sits in the parent's books.
Upstream — subsidiary sells to parent. Profit sits in the subsidiary's books.
Controlling interest only. The deferral/realization is attributed entirely to the parent. The NCI income computation is NOT adjusted.
Shared with the NCI. Because the profit is the subsidiary's, the deferral and later realization change the subsidiary's realized income — so they flow into the NCI income computation at the NCI %.

Asset type

Inventory — resold or consumed. Entries TI, G, *G.
Land — not depreciated. Entries TL, *GL.
Depreciable asset — equipment/buildings. Entries TA, ED.
Realizes quickly. Deferred profit in ending inventory is recognized next period when the goods are sold/consumed outside.
Deferral persists. With no depreciation, the deferred gain stays on the worksheet every year until the land is sold to an outsider.
Unwinds gradually. The buyer over-depreciates the inflated basis, so each year's "excess depreciation" recognizes a slice of the deferred gain over the remaining life.
For Pullman / Spencer: inventory is UP (Spencer→Pullman), while land and equipment are DOWN (Pullman→Spencer). So only the inventory deferrals touch the NCI; the land and equipment deferrals are all the parent's.

Acquisition recap: excess, allocation, amortization TEXTBOOK

Carried from Ch.4 — you still need these for Entries A, E, and the NCI

The intra-entity work sits on top of the normal consolidation. Rebuild the day-one allocation: total fair value (consideration + NCI fair value) minus book value is the excess; assign it to identifiable items; the residual is goodwill or an indefinite intangible. Then amortize the finite-lived pieces.

How the textbook builds these (LO 4 carryover)

Total FV = consideration + NCI fair value; Excess = Total FV − book value; assign to identifiable accounts, residual to goodwill or an indefinite intangible; Annual amortization = finite allocation ÷ life. The indefinite-life trademark and goodwill are never amortized. This annual amortization reduces the subsidiary's adjusted income and therefore also the NCI's income share.

How the textbook builds these (LO 5-2 → 5-4)

  • Gross profit rate = (transfer price − cost) ÷ transfer price. Apply it to the inventory still held at year-end (stated at transfer price) to get the unrealized profit.
  • Entry TI removes the intra-entity Sales and the matching Cost of Goods Sold — they net to zero for the combined entity, but both totals must come off.
  • Entry G defers the current-year profit still in ending inventory: debit COGS (lowering consolidated income) and credit Inventory (removing the markup from the balance sheet).
  • Entry *G is entered first because it fixes a beginning balance: it removes last year's deferred profit from the seller's beginning Retained Earnings and credits COGS so the now-realized profit lands in this year's income.
  • Who is the "seller"? Downstream → parent's Retained Earnings; upstream → subsidiary's Retained Earnings (which is why upstream touches the NCI).

How the textbook handles land (LO 5-6)

  • Gain = transfer price − original cost. In the year of transfer, Entry TL removes the recorded "Gain on Sale of Land" and restores Land to its original cost.
  • Every subsequent year the land is still held, Entry *GL repeats the deferral — but now the debit is to the seller's beginning Retained Earnings (the gain is in a prior period), still crediting Land.
  • Year of outside sale: the deferred gain is finally recognized — debit the seller's beginning Retained Earnings, credit Gain on Sale of Land — so the full profit hits consolidated income that year.
  • Pullman→Spencer makes this downstream, so the whole deferral belongs to the parent and the NCI is untouched.

How the textbook handles depreciable assets (LO 5-7)

  • Gain = transfer price − book value at transfer; excess depreciation = gain ÷ remaining life.
  • Entry TA restores the asset and accumulated depreciation to their original-cost amounts and removes the gain. In the year of transfer the offset is "Gain on Sale"; in later years it's the seller's beginning Retained Earnings, reduced by the excess depreciation already recognized (gain − annual × prior years).
  • Entry ED removes the current year's over-depreciation: debit Accumulated Depreciation, credit Depreciation Expense — this is how the deferred gain is recognized, one annual slice at a time, over the remaining life.
  • Pullman→Spencer makes this downstream, so the gain and its annual unwind belong entirely to the parent; the NCI is untouched.
N

The noncontrolling-interest income computation NCI

LO 5-5 — only UPSTREAM transfers change this

The NCI gets its percentage of the subsidiary's realized income. Start from the subsidiary's reported income, subtract excess amortization, then adjust for upstream deferrals only: subtract this year's deferred profit and add back last year's profit now realized. Downstream transfers do not appear here.

NCI percentage %
Subsidiary reported net income
− Excess fair-value amortization
− Current-year UPSTREAM profit deferred (ending inventory, etc.)
+ Prior-year UPSTREAM profit now realized (beginning inventory, etc.)
= Subsidiary realized net income—
= Net income attributable to NCI (× NCI %)—

How the textbook builds the NCI income (LO 5-5)

NCI income = NCI % × (sub reported income − excess amortization − current upstream deferral + prior upstream realization).

  • Why only upstream? Upstream profit lives in the subsidiary's income, so deferring/realizing it changes the subsidiary's realized income — which is what the NCI shares. Downstream profit is the parent's, so it never enters this line.
  • Year of transfer: subtract the deferred amount (income the subsidiary recorded but hasn't truly earned).
  • Year of realization: add it back (income now genuinely earned).
  • Cross-check: the same upstream adjustments flow through the consolidated income statement; the NCI simply takes its percentage of the adjusted subsidiary figure.

Reliability checklist STUDY AID

A self-verification routine — habits that catch the common transfer errors

The textbook gives the method; this checklist makes it reliable. Tick each before calling the problem done.

0 of 8 complete
  • I classified every transfer by direction (down/up) and type (inventory/land/depreciable) before computing.
  • Gross profit rate used the transfer price as the base, and I applied it to inventory still held at year-end.
  • Entry *G / *GL came first and adjusted the seller's beginning Retained Earnings.
  • Entry TI removed both intra-entity Sales and the matching Cost of Goods Sold.
  • Land deferral repeats every year (no unwind); equipment deferral unwinds via excess depreciation.
  • Only UPSTREAM deferrals/realizations entered the NCI income computation; downstream did not.
  • I eliminated the intra-entity receivable/payable (Entry P) for the unpaid transfer.
  • Investment nets to zero; intra-entity sales, gains, and inflated bases are all removed.
